01 Context

There are approximately 466 million people worldwide who have disabling hearing loss. Many of them still interested in music want to experience it. In fact, many of them are exploring music with other senses and some even become professional musician/dancers.However, most deaf people still found hard to experience music.

How might we deliver musical experiences to people with hearing disabilities through other sense?
video from AJ+'s Youtube Channel on a Deaf dancer and instructor with his own method of feeling music's vibrations to learn a song.

02 Research

Although people who have disabling hearing loss have never actually 'heard' music, their bodies and ears are able to pick up different sound waves, vibration and low notes. Dr. Dean Shibata, a professor in the Department of Radiology at the University of Washington, published his music-related findings. "The Brain is incredibly adaptable. In someone who is deaf, the young brain takes advantage of valuable real estate in the brain by processing vibrations in the parts of the brain that would otherwise be used to process sound," Shibata shares.

In addition to the use of vibration, the ASL (American Sign Language) music "movement" is another approach to harmoniously bridge a hearing-centric experience over to a visually-centric experience. “I do at least forty to fifty events per year, and that includes festivals which may include four shows a day,” says Kelly Kurdi, a multilingual interpreter with Amber G. Productions who studied at Gallaudet University, an institution for the deaf.

Amber suggests that solely signing a song’s words and, then, pausing during instrumentals is an injustice. She’s teaching others how to fully immerse deaf music lovers through expression and emotion ─ and without missing a beat in a drum solo! Kelly demonstrates in the following video:

03 Diagnosis

According to the research, we've found the opportunity to create a product that uses vibrations and visual contents to bring musical experience to people with disabling hearing disabilities, which is based on these guiding policies:
‍

Intuitive

To provide intuitive and user-friendly interaction

Engaging

To deliver engaging content and delightful experience

Encouraging

To create a community that encourages more ASL contents
