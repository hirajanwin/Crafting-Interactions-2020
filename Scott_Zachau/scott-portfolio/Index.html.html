Overview

As a part of SVA's Interaction Design MFA Crafting Interactions course, IÂ chose to explore how a blood pressure monitoring wearable and app would function in the future.

My primary goal was to design an easy to use system that could help user's gain a deeper understanding of how their behavior affects their blood pressure.

Project Overview

Information Architecture

To kick-off my design, I outlined the Mobile app and watch interface's information architecture concurrent path. My goal was for each modality to work in symphony with one another.

Site Map

The site map of the mobile phone and app show how the two modalities work together. I wanted the primary use of the watch to be for measurement and the mobile app for data analysis. For this reason, the watches functionality is limited in comparison to the mobile app.

Blood Pressure App Wireframe

After outlining the site maps, IÂ dug into the wireframes. The app is minimalist and simple -- there are only three primary navigation points. My goal was to build upon the mental model of a notebook.

Watch Blood Pressure Wirefame

I spent the majority of my effort focusing on the 3 step blood pressure measurement process. Obtaining a correct blood pressure reading requires a number of conditions to be met and I wanted those to be clear to the user.

High Fidelity V1

After completing the wireframes, I designed the first high fidelity version of the blood pressure app. The inaugural version included activity and weight tracking to provide a more holistic view of the user's life.

User Research

I built an interactive prototype in XDÂ to test my first version of the app. Due to the Corona virus lock down I was only able to test with one user. fortunately, that one user, my fiance is studying to be a physicians assistant!

My primary goals were to:Â 

  • To learn what cloud platforms exist, their primary use cases, and the possibility of extracting data from said platforms.
  • To understand the current player's networking/resourcing behaviors, processes and current pain points.

Below are the major points of feedback that emerged out of my user research.

"67 Year olds are not tracking their workouts"

In my research, IÂ learned that the typical 60+ high blood pressure patient does not "workout." They are more likely to walk and track their steps. After learning this, I realized that the activity and weight tracker only made the app more confusing so I deleted it.

"When was the reading taken"

The first version of the design did not clearly state when the last BPÂ and HRÂ measurement was taken. In the second iteration of the design I remedied this issue.

"It's great that you can compare day and night readings"

In my primary research, I learned that a person's blood pressure readings vary throughout the day and night. To get a proper measurement, patients are recommended to measure their BPÂ both in the day and night. As a practitioner, My fiance loved that I acknowledged this truth in my design.

"I love the log and diagnosis"

As a practitioner, my fiance loved the log view and the fact that the app offered a diagnosis on the measurement. Often times, patients don't have a great understanding of what their readings really mean.

High Fidelity V2

Upon review of our research findings, I redesigned the blood pressure app and watch interfaces. The second edition is minimalist in function and provides clarity around when the measurements were taken.
